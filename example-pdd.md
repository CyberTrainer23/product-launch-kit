# PDD Reference: Product Description Document Format

> **HOW TO USE THIS FILE**
> This is a real-world example of a Product Description Document (PDD). When generating a new PDD, follow this document's structure, section order, and level of detail exactly. The PDD was created by and for the marketing department — every section answers a question marketing will be asked. Replace all example content with answers from the user's questionnaire. Produce one document per phase.
>
> **CRITICAL — Section Headers:** The following headers are fixed and must be copied verbatim every time. Do not rename, reword, reframe, or creatively substitute any of them — even if a different framing feels more appropriate for the product being described:
> - `## 1. Product Overview`
> - `## 2. Strategic Context`
> - `## 3. Audience & Use Case`
> - `## 4. Product Details`
> - `## 5. Positioning Inputs`
> - `## 6. Metrics & Validation`
> - `## 7. Launch Preparation`
> - `## 8. Language Guidelines`
>
> ---
>
> **VOICE AND PERSONA — Read before writing a single word**
>
> The PDD is a marketing brief written by a strategist who has done the work. The primary audience is the marketing team. Every section exists because marketing asked for it. Write accordingly.
>
> **Primary voice: The confident strategist.**
> Has a clear point of view and states it. Makes positioning calls and backs them up with evidence. Does not hedge conclusions with "may be perceived as" or "could potentially." When a competitor is weak on something, says so plainly. When a product has a genuine advantage, names it specifically — not generically.
>
> **Secondary voice: The brand voice guardian.**
> Every word choice is deliberate. The language guidelines section is not a list of rules — it is a brief on how the customer actually talks and what impression each word creates. The positioning statement is not a fill-in-the-blank exercise — it is a staked claim that marketing can build a campaign from.
>
> **Voice by section:**
>
> - **Section 1 — Product Overview:** Factual and complete. A non-technical reader should be able to explain this product to a prospect after reading the summary. No jargon.
>
> - **Section 2 — Strategic Context:** States the business case plainly. Not "there is an opportunity to" — just: here's the problem, here's why it matters now, here's what we're doing about it. Competitive landscape is direct — names competitors, names their weaknesses, names our advantage. No diplomatic softening.
>
> - **Section 3 — Audience & Use Case:** Written from the customer's perspective. The persona description should sound like a real person's Tuesday, not a demographic profile. Use cases are specific enough that a sales rep could use them as conversation starters verbatim.
>
> - **Section 4 — Product Details:** Specific and honest. Features are described in terms of what they do for the user, not how they were built. Differentiators name what competitors lack — not what we have in the abstract. If a dependency or limitation exists, state it.
>
> - **Section 5 — Positioning Inputs:** The most important section for marketing. Written like a brief to a copywriter — clear and specific enough that they could write a headline from it without a follow-up meeting. The entry point is written in the customer's exact vocabulary — the words they use before they know this product exists.
>
> - **Section 6 — Metrics & Validation:** Specific and honest. Cite real numbers where they exist. If data is from a pilot or projection, say so. Do not present estimates as validated metrics.
>
> - **Section 7 — Launch Preparation:** Practical and complete. Dates, owners, and material descriptions. Marketing needs to know what exists, when it will be ready, and where to get it.
>
> - **Section 8 — Language Guidelines:** Written from the customer's perspective. Do's use the words customers actually use. Don'ts explain the exact impression the wrong word creates — not just "avoid this" but "this implies X which undermines Y." Every item in this section should make a copywriter's job easier, not harder.
>
> **What this voice never does:**
> - Uses passive voice to avoid taking a position
> - Says "leverage" as a verb
> - Writes "unique" without explaining what makes it unique
> - Uses "solution" as a noun when a specific product name exists
> - Opens a section with "In today's competitive landscape"
> - Writes a positioning statement that could apply to any product in the category
> - Softens a competitive advantage with "we believe" or "we feel"
> - Lists a differentiator that the competitor also has
> - `## 5. Positioning Inputs`
> - `## 6. Metrics & Validation`
> - `## 7. Launch Preparation`
> - `## 8. Language Guidelines`

# Product Description Document (PDD)

*This document captures essential information about a new product or feature release. It ensures alignment across teams and provides the context needed for messaging, positioning, and go-to-market planning.*

---

## 1. Product Overview

- **Product Name:** Slack Scheduled Messages

- **Planned Launch Date:** November 2, 2020

- **Primary Contact(s):** Sarah K., Product Manager — Messaging Foundations

- **Summary:**
  Scheduled Messages lets Slack users compose a message now and choose exactly when it gets delivered — later today, tomorrow morning, or any future date and time. The sender controls the timing; the recipient experiences it as a normal message. This gives teams a better way to communicate across time zones without worrying about bothering colleagues at odd hours.

- **Platform Context:**
  Scheduled Messages builds on Slack's core messaging composer. It complements existing features like Do Not Disturb and notification preferences, giving the sender — not just the recipient — control over communication timing. It's a direct enhancement to the async communication experience that defines Slack's value for distributed teams.

---

## 2. Strategic Context

- **Business Challenge / Problem Statement:**
  Distributed teams increasingly work across time zones, but Slack's messaging model has always been synchronous by default. Users who work outside traditional business hours face a social pressure problem: they compose messages late at night or early in the morning but hesitate to send them, fearing they'll disrupt colleagues. This leads to message abandonment, missed context, and workarounds (drafting in Notes apps, setting phone reminders). The result is communication friction that undermines Slack's core promise of making work life simpler.

- **Customer Opportunities Unlocked:**
  - Teams with global members can share updates at a time convenient for the recipient, not just the sender.
  - Individual contributors can work when they're productive without signaling unavailability expectations to teammates.
  - Managers can prepare and queue team updates in advance, reducing context-switching.

- **Market Segment:**
  Primary: Mid-market and enterprise customers (500+ employees) with distributed or international teams. Secondary: Any Slack paid customer who frequently works outside their recipients' business hours.

- **Competitive Landscape:**
  - **Microsoft Teams** launched scheduled send in Q1 2020 and uses it as a selling point in enterprise deals.
  - **Gmail** has offered scheduled send since 2018; users frequently cite it as a feature they expect from messaging tools.
  - **No other team messaging tool** currently offers this at parity or better.
  Slack's differentiation: tighter integration with timezone awareness (surfacing the recipient's local time at point of scheduling) and a dedicated Scheduled Messages management view.

- **Strategic Objective:**
  Close a competitive feature gap with Teams. Support Slack's positioning as the async-first communication platform for distributed teams. Directly addresses a top-3 most-requested feature from enterprise customers, supporting renewal and expansion conversations.

---

## 3. Audience & Use Case

- **Primary Persona(s):**
  *The Distributed Team Lead* — A manager or senior IC at a 500–5,000 person company with team members in 2+ time zones. They use Slack heavily for daily communication and often work outside standard hours. They are thoughtful about communication norms and feel genuine friction around sending messages at odd hours.

- **Audience Details:**

  | Attribute | Detail |
  |-----------|--------|
  | Role | Manager, Senior IC, PM, Engineering Lead |
  | Industry | Technology, Professional Services, Financial Services |
  | Org Size | 500–10,000 employees |
  | Pain Points | Can't communicate on their own schedule without appearing intrusive; workarounds are clunky and unreliable |
  | Opportunities | Async-first workflows; respecting timezone norms; work-life balance signaling |

- **Key Use Cases / Pain Points Addressed:**
  1. Composing a message after hours that should be read at the start of the recipient's business day
  2. Preparing team updates in advance to send at a consistent time (e.g., Monday 9am recaps)
  3. Sending reminders or follow-ups at a future time without relying on personal calendar reminders

- **Audience Applicability:**
  Available to all Slack paid plan customers (Pro, Business+, Enterprise Grid) on desktop at launch. Free plan customers are excluded from v1. Mobile support to follow in a subsequent release.

---

## 4. Product Details

- **Core Features & Functionality:**
  1. **Schedule Send** — A secondary action on the send button in the message composer. Users select a preset time (Tomorrow morning, Monday morning) or enter a custom date and time.
  2. **Timezone Awareness** — If the recipient's timezone is set in their Slack profile, it is surfaced as a tooltip during scheduling ("That's 3am for @rami").
  3. **Scheduled Messages View** — A dedicated management view (accessible from the sidebar) showing all pending scheduled messages, with options to edit or cancel each one.
  4. **Edit & Cancel** — Any scheduled message can be modified or cancelled up until the moment of delivery.

- **Solution Overview:**
  Scheduled Messages integrates directly into the existing message composer with minimal UI addition — a dropdown arrow on the send button reveals the scheduling option. Once scheduled, the message is queued and delivered at the specified time exactly as if the user had sent it manually in that moment. Recipients see a normal message with no indicator that it was scheduled.

- **Key Benefits:**
  - **For senders:** Removes the social pressure of sending at odd hours. Work when you're productive; communicate when it's respectful.
  - **For recipients:** Receive messages at the start of their day with full context, reducing the cognitive load of waking up to a late-night message thread.
  - **For teams:** Supports healthier async norms without requiring process changes or policies.

- **Differentiators:**
  - Native timezone awareness built into the scheduling UI (competitors require manual calculation)
  - Dedicated management view for all scheduled messages in one place
  - No recipient-visible indicator — maintains the natural feel of Slack conversation

- **Dependencies / Integrations:**
  - Requires Slack's existing notification infrastructure for delivery timing
  - Profile timezone data integration for timezone tooltip feature
  - No third-party dependencies

- **Market Context:**
  Async communication tools is a growing segment within team collaboration software. The rise of distributed work (accelerated by COVID-19) has made time zone management a core product concern for all collaboration platforms. Scheduled Messages positions Slack in the async-first category alongside email.

- **Pricing / Packaging:**
  Included in all paid Slack plans (Pro, Business+, Enterprise Grid) at no additional cost. Not available on the Free plan.

---

## 5. Positioning Inputs

- **Intended Market Position:**
  Thoughtful, async-first. This is not a "power user feature" — it's a considerate communication tool that respects the boundaries of distributed teams.

- **Competitive Differentiators:**
  - Unlike Teams, Slack surfaces the recipient's local time at the point of scheduling
  - Unlike Gmail, Slack's scheduling is built for team conversations, not just 1:1 emails
  - Dedicated Scheduled Messages view makes it easy to manage multiple queued messages

- **Entry Point:**
  Customers describe this problem as: *"I don't want to send a Slack at midnight but I always forget in the morning"* or *"I work with people in London and I never know when to message them."*

- **Positioning Statement:**
  For distributed teams who need to communicate across time zones without disrupting colleagues' personal time, Slack Scheduled Messages is the async communication feature that lets you send messages on your schedule while respecting others' boundaries. Unlike email scheduling or manual workarounds, it's built natively into the conversations your team already uses every day.

- **Value Summary:**
  Work when you're most productive. Communicate when it's most respectful.

- **Launch Type:** New feature launch (General Availability)

---

## 6. Metrics & Validation

- **Expected Impact:**
  - **Adoption:** 20% of active desktop users schedule at least one message within 30 days of launch
  - **Delivery success rate:** >99.9% of scheduled messages delivered within 60 seconds of scheduled time
  - **Retention signal:** Feature users show +5% higher 90-day retention vs. matched non-users
  - **Competitive:** Reduction in "missing scheduled send" mentions in competitive deal notes

- **Customer or Beta Feedback:**
  Pilot with 50 internal Slack employees showed strong adoption (78% used at least once in 2 weeks). Top feedback: "I use it every day for my Berlin team." One edge case surfaced: users wanted to know if a message was delivered successfully — addressed with a delivery confirmation in the Scheduled Messages view.

- **Known Dependencies:**
  Notification infrastructure must be able to handle scheduled delivery reliably at scale. Load testing completed for 10x current message volume — no issues surfaced.

## 8. Language Guidelines

- **Do's:**
  - Use: "Scheduled Messages" (capitalized, as a product feature name)
  - Use: "send later", "schedule your message", "choose when your message is delivered"
  - Emphasize: respecting teammates' time, working across time zones, async communication
  - Lead with the benefit to the sender and recipient, not the feature mechanics

- **Don'ts:**
  - Do not use: "delay send" — implies the message is being withheld, not intentionally timed
  - Do not use: "queue" — too technical, not customer language
  - Do not say: "your message will be stored and sent later" — raises unnecessary concerns about data/privacy
  - Avoid implying this is for hiding when you're working — the positioning is about consideration, not concealment
