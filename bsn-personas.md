# BSN Default Personas

> **HOW TO USE THIS FILE**
> These are the default BSN personas used across all product launch skill phases. When a phase requires persona simulation, present this list to the user first and ask if any changes are needed before proceeding. Do not ask the user to select personas from scratch — present these as the default set and allow additions, removals, or edits on request.
>
> **Default behavior:** Use all 8 personas unless the user specifies otherwise. For products that clearly don't apply to a specific segment (e.g., a feature only relevant to enterprise MSPs), flag which personas may not be applicable and ask the user to confirm before excluding them.

---

## How to Present Personas at the Start of a Phase

Before running any persona simulation, present the following summary and ask for confirmation:

> "I'll be running this simulation using BSN's 8 default personas across small, mid-market, and large MSPs, plus SMB end clients. Here's who I'll be using:
>
> 1. Marcus Webb — Solo MSP Owner (Small)
> 2. Dana Chu — Technical Lead (Small MSP)
> 3. Rachel Okonkwo — Owner/GM (Mid-Market MSP)
> 4. Tyler Barnett — Sales Rep (Mid-Market MSP)
> 5. Greg Palermo — VP of Sales (Large MSP)
> 6. Simone Delacroix — Operations/Service Manager (Large MSP)
> 7. Janet Howell — Office/Operations Manager (SMB Client)
> 8. Carlos Medina — HR/Compliance Manager (SMB Client)
>
> Would you like to add, remove, or edit any personas before I begin?"

Wait for confirmation before proceeding.

## Persona 1 — Marcus Webb
**Segment:** Small MSP (1–10 technicians)
**Role:** Owner / Founder

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Company size | 3–8 technicians |
| Industry | Generalist MSP — serves local SMBs across multiple verticals |
| Location | Mid-size US city, often suburban or rural market |
| Seniority | Owner — sole decision maker |

**Day in the life:**
Marcus is in the weeds every day. He's answering support tickets in the morning, on a sales call at lunch, and reviewing invoices at night. He has no dedicated sales or marketing staff. He found BSN because a peer in his peer group mentioned it, or he saw it at an industry event. He evaluates tools based on one question: "Will this help me make more money without creating more work for me?"

**Primary pain points:**
- Not enough hours in the day to manage clients AND grow the business
- Clients don't see the value of security training until something goes wrong
- Hard to prove ROI to small business owners who think they're "too small to be a target"
- Renewal conversations are awkward without data to back up the value

**Goals:**
- Add recurring revenue without adding headcount
- Retain clients by becoming indispensable
- Stay competitive against larger MSPs pitching the same clients

**How they buy:**
Self-serve or low-touch sales. Will sign up for a trial, explore on their own, and make a decision quickly. Doesn't have time for long evaluation cycles. Price sensitivity is high — needs to see a clear path to margin.

**Who influences them:**
Peer groups (HTG, CompTIA communities), vendor reps they trust, other MSP owners in their network.

**Vocabulary:**
"I need something I can set and forget." / "My clients don't get it until it's too late." / "I can't add another tool that takes 10 hours to learn." / "What's my margin on this?"

**Biggest fears / objections:**
- Too complex to manage on top of everything else
- Clients will push back on the cost
- Another tool that promises results but doesn't deliver
- Gets locked into a contract with a vendor that doesn't support them

## Persona 2 — Dana Chu
**Segment:** Small MSP (1–10 technicians)
**Role:** Technical Lead / Senior Technician

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Company size | 3–8 technicians |
| Industry | Generalist MSP |
| Location | Works remotely or in-office at small MSP |
| Seniority | Senior technician — reports to owner |

**Day in the life:**
Dana is the person who actually implements and manages everything the owner decides to buy. She evaluates tools for technical fit, manages integrations, and is the first call when something breaks. She's skeptical of anything that creates extra work or requires babysitting. She often has more influence over tool decisions than her title suggests — if Dana says it's too complicated, Marcus won't buy it.

**Primary pain points:**
- Tools that promise automation but require constant manual intervention
- Platforms with poor documentation or slow support
- Clients who don't complete their training, making reporting look bad
- Managing multiple client environments with different configurations

**Goals:**
- Tools that integrate cleanly into the existing stack (PSA, RMM)
- Automation that actually works — set it, forget it
- Clear reporting she can hand to Marcus without building it herself
- Vendors who respond quickly when something breaks

**How they buy:**
Doesn't own the buying decision but heavily influences it. Will run a trial, document what she finds, and give Marcus a thumbs up or down. Looks for technical documentation, integration guides, and peer reviews on Reddit or Spiceworks.

**Who influences them:**
Tech communities (Reddit MSP, Spiceworks), vendor technical support, other technicians in peer networks.

**Vocabulary:**
"Does it integrate with ConnectWise?" / "Who do I call when it breaks?" / "The last platform said it was automated but I still had to touch everything." / "I need to see the API docs."

**Biggest fears / objections:**
- Poor support response times
- Integrations that are brittle or require workarounds
- Clients who ignore training and make the platform look ineffective
- Being the one blamed when a tool the owner bought doesn't work

## Persona 3 — Rachel Okonkwo
**Segment:** Mid-Market MSP (11–50 technicians)
**Role:** Owner / General Manager

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Company size | 15–40 technicians |
| Industry | Generalist or vertically focused MSP (healthcare, legal, financial services) |
| Location | Regional US market — often serving a metro area |
| Seniority | Owner or GM — sets strategy, manages leadership team |

**Day in the life:**
Rachel has stepped back from day-to-day technical work and is focused on growth, margins, and building a scalable business. She has a small leadership team — a service manager, a sales lead, and a few senior techs. She evaluates vendors based on strategic fit, not just feature lists. She attends IT Nation, ASCII, or similar events and is plugged into the MSP community. She's heard BSN's name and is evaluating whether it's worth adding to her stack.

**Primary pain points:**
- Differentiating her MSP in a crowded market
- Clients increasingly asking about compliance (especially HIPAA) and she needs a credible answer
- Sales team struggles to articulate the value of security training without sounding alarmist
- Needs tools that scale across 50–200 client endpoints without creating service desk chaos

**Goals:**
- Position her MSP as a trusted advisor, not just a break-fix vendor
- Add a recurring revenue stream that doesn't require significant technical overhead
- Win more vertically-focused clients (healthcare, legal) where compliance is table stakes
- Retain clients longer by deepening the relationship

**How they buy:**
Vendor-led with internal evaluation. Will involve her service manager and sales lead in the decision. Wants a business case, not just a demo. Looks for proof points — case studies, peer references, ROI data.

**Who influences them:**
Peer group leaders, vendor partner managers, industry analysts, other MSP owners at her size.

**Vocabulary:**
"How does this help me differentiate?" / "My sales team needs to be able to explain this in 30 seconds." / "What does the onboarding look like at scale?" / "Show me the numbers."

**Biggest fears / objections:**
- Adding a product her team can't sell confidently
- Onboarding complexity that creates service desk tickets instead of revenue
- Vendor that doesn't invest in her success after the contract is signed
- Clients who see training as a checkbox and not a real service

## Persona 4 — Tyler Barnett
**Segment:** Mid-Market MSP (11–50 technicians)
**Role:** Account Manager / Sales Rep

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Company size | 15–40 technicians |
| Industry | Generalist MSP |
| Location | US regional market |
| Seniority | Individual contributor — reports to owner or sales manager |

**Day in the life:**
Tyler manages a book of existing clients and is responsible for upselling and cross-selling. He's not a technical person — he relies on the service team to explain how things work. His job is to get clients to say yes to additional services. He's comfortable talking about cybersecurity in general terms but struggles to explain why training is worth the extra line item when clients already feel like they're paying enough.

**Primary pain points:**
- Clients push back on price for training — "We already have antivirus, why do we need this?"
- Hard to explain the difference between BSN and competitors without getting into the weeds
- No good leave-behind material that explains the value without requiring a tech to be in the room
- Hitting quota means finding upsell opportunities that are easy to close, not hard to explain

**Goals:**
- Simple talking points that close the training conversation quickly
- Marketing materials he can send before or after a meeting
- Proof points — breaches, statistics, client success stories — that create urgency
- Make the upsell feel like a natural extension of what the client already has

**How they buy:**
Doesn't make vendor decisions but influences them by flagging what's sellable. Will champion a product internally if he believes he can sell it. Looks for sales enablement materials, competitive comparisons, and objection handling guides.

**Who influences them:**
His manager, BSN partner success reps, peer sales reps at other MSPs, client feedback.

**Vocabulary:**
"How do I explain this to a client who doesn't think they're a target?" / "What's the one thing I lead with?" / "My clients are already paying a lot — how do I justify adding this?" / "Give me something I can send them."

**Biggest fears / objections:**
- Clients saying no and him not having a good response
- Product that's hard to explain without a technical person in the room
- Being oversold on capabilities that don't match what the client experiences
- Losing a client who sees the upsell as a money grab

## Persona 5 — Greg Palermo
**Segment:** Large MSP (50+ technicians)
**Role:** VP of Sales

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Company size | 75–300 technicians |
| Industry | Multi-vertical MSP or national/regional brand |
| Location | US metro market or national footprint |
| Seniority | VP — reports to CEO/COO, manages sales team of 5-20 reps |

**Day in the life:**
Greg manages a sales team and is responsible for hitting revenue targets across new business and client retention. He's data-driven, attends QBRs with major clients, and is accountable to a board or ownership group. He doesn't evaluate tools himself — he has managers for that. He cares about MRR impact, sales cycle length, competitive win rates, and whether a vendor will actually help his team sell. He's seen a lot of vendors promise results and underdeliver.

**Primary pain points:**
- Sales team inconsistency — some reps can sell training confidently, others avoid the conversation
- Difficult to scale a training pitch that doesn't rely on a technical person being in the room
- Clients at this size have procurement processes — needs vendor materials that survive a formal review
- Needs proof that adding BSN to the stack actually increases MRR, not just client complexity

**Goals:**
- Increase average revenue per client through recurring training services
- Shorten the sales cycle on security training conversations
- Arm his team with materials that work without him in the room
- Maintain competitive positioning against other large MSPs and direct vendors

**How they buy:**
Formal vendor evaluation. Will involve procurement, legal, and operations in the decision. Wants executive-level presentations, SLA documentation, and ROI models. Expects a dedicated partner manager and executive support from BSN.

**Who influences them:**
CEO/COO, peer VPs at other MSPs, industry analysts, BSN executive team.

**Vocabulary:**
"What's the MRR impact per client?" / "How does this shorten my sales cycle?" / "My team needs to be able to sell this without me." / "What does your partner program actually include?" / "Show me a win/loss analysis."

**Biggest fears / objections:**
- Vendor that doesn't scale support as the relationship grows
- Tool that creates more work for the sales team than it saves
- Clients pushing back during formal procurement reviews
- BSN going direct and competing with his team for end clients

## Persona 6 — Simone Delacroix
**Segment:** Large MSP (50+ technicians)
**Role:** Operations / Service Delivery Manager

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Company size | 75–300 technicians |
| Industry | Multi-vertical MSP |
| Location | US metro or national footprint |
| Seniority | Mid-senior — reports to COO or VP of Operations |

**Day in the life:**
Simone owns the service delivery engine. She manages the tool stack, vendor relationships, onboarding processes, and service desk efficiency. When a new vendor is added, she's the one who has to make it work across 100+ clients without disrupting the team. She's detail-oriented, process-driven, and deeply skeptical of vendors who oversell ease of implementation. She's the one who will approve or kill a BSN rollout based on how well it integrates with existing workflows.

**Primary pain points:**
- New tools that require custom configuration per client instead of scalable templates
- Vendor onboarding that takes months instead of days
- Integrations that break when PSA or RMM platforms update
- Reporting that doesn't match what the sales team promises clients

**Goals:**
- Standardized onboarding process that can be delegated to a junior tech
- Clean integrations with ConnectWise, Autotask, or Datto
- Reporting that requires minimal manual effort to produce
- A vendor that treats her team as partners, not just ticket numbers

**How they buy:**
Leads the technical evaluation and implementation planning. Has veto power over the tool decision. Will run a structured pilot before committing. Requires documentation, integration guides, and a dedicated technical onboarding contact.

**Who influences them:**
COO/VP Ops, service desk leads, PSA/RMM vendor communities, BSN technical support team.

**Vocabulary:**
"How long does onboarding actually take?" / "Does this play nice with ConnectWise?" / "Who's my technical contact when something breaks?" / "Can I templatize this across all my clients or does it have to be done one at a time?"

**Biggest fears / objections:**
- Implementation that takes longer than promised and pulls resources from the service desk
- Integrations that require ongoing maintenance
- Vendor support that routes everything through a helpdesk instead of a named contact
- Being the one accountable when a rollout goes poorly

## Persona 7 — Janet Howell
**Segment:** SMB End Client
**Role:** Office Manager / Operations Manager

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Company size | 20–100 employees |
| Industry | Professional services, healthcare, legal, real estate, or light manufacturing |
| Location | US regional market |
| Seniority | Mid-level — reports to owner or COO |

**Day in the life:**
Janet runs the office. She handles HR, vendor relationships, compliance paperwork, and anything that doesn't fit neatly into someone else's job description. She was asked by the owner to "handle the cybersecurity training thing" after their MSP mentioned it — or after a close call with a phishing email. She is not technical. She doesn't know what a PSA is. She needs training to be simple, fast to deploy, and easy to report on when the owner asks how it's going.

**Primary pain points:**
- Employees who ignore or rush through training
- No way to prove to the owner that the training is actually working
- Not sure what compliance actually requires — HIPAA, state laws, insurance requirements feel overwhelming
- Being blamed if something goes wrong, even though she's not the IT person

**Goals:**
- Get employees trained with minimal disruption to their workday
- Have something to show the owner — completion rates, risk scores, anything
- Feel confident that the company is doing what it's supposed to do from a compliance standpoint
- Not have to think about it constantly — set it up and let it run

**How they buy:**
Doesn't make the vendor decision — her MSP does. But she's the one who has to use the platform day-to-day and report on it to ownership. If the platform is confusing or creates work for her, she will complain to the MSP, which puts the MSP's relationship at risk.

**Who influences them:**
Her MSP account manager, the business owner, peers in her industry, insurance providers who ask about cybersecurity coverage.

**Vocabulary:**
"I just need to make sure everyone does their training." / "The owner wants to know we're covered." / "I don't have time to chase people down every week." / "What do I do if someone doesn't complete it?"

**Biggest fears / objections:**
- Training that takes too long and disrupts the workday
- Employees who refuse or complain about the training
- Not being able to explain to the owner why it matters
- Something going wrong and being blamed because she was in charge of it

## Persona 8 — Carlos Medina
**Segment:** SMB End Client
**Role:** HR Manager / Compliance Officer

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Company size | 50–250 employees |
| Industry | Healthcare, financial services, legal, or any regulated vertical |
| Location | US regional or national |
| Seniority | Mid-senior — reports to COO, CFO, or legal counsel |

**Day in the life:**
Carlos owns compliance at his organization. He manages HIPAA training, maintains policy documentation, prepares for audits, and coordinates with legal and IT on anything that touches employee data or security. He's more sophisticated than Janet — he understands regulatory requirements and takes them seriously. He came to BSN (or his MSP came to him) because his current training program is a generic LMS that employees ignore and auditors barely accept.

**Primary pain points:**
- Training that employees treat as a checkbox — click through, forget immediately
- Generic compliance content that doesn't reflect the real threats their industry faces
- Audit prep that requires manually pulling data from multiple systems
- Keeping up with changing regulations without a dedicated compliance team

**Goals:**
- Training that employees actually engage with and retain
- Reporting that satisfies auditors without manual data assembly
- Content that stays current with evolving threats and regulations
- A defensible record of employee training in case of a breach or audit

**How they buy:**
Influences or owns the vendor decision depending on company size. Will evaluate platforms based on compliance coverage, content quality, and reporting depth. May involve IT, legal, or procurement. Wants a demo focused on compliance use cases, not general cybersecurity awareness.

**Who influences them:**
Legal counsel, insurance providers, industry compliance bodies (HIPAA, state regulators), peer HR/compliance professionals, IT leadership.

**Vocabulary:**
"Does this satisfy HIPAA training requirements?" / "What does the audit trail look like?" / "Our employees click through everything — does this actually change behavior?" / "How current is the content?" / "What happens if there's a breach — what documentation do I have?"

**Biggest fears / objections:**
- Training that doesn't hold up under regulatory scrutiny
- Content that becomes outdated between annual renewals
- Employees who complete training but still fall for phishing — making the training look ineffective
- A breach happening on his watch despite having a training program in place
